## Prioritize the reader

Reserve clear, responsive ad areas without shifting content. Keep article text and navigation easy to use.

## Review program policies

Follow current platform rules, disclose data practices, and avoid misleading placements or accidental clicks.

## Why AdSense makes sense for a tech blog

If you write about code, tools, tutorials, or anything developer-related, you already have the hardest part done — content that solves real problems for real people. AdSense is Google's way of turning that traffic into a small, steady income stream without you having to sell anything or chase sponsors.

It's not going to make you rich overnight. Most bloggers earn a modest amount per thousand visitors, and that number depends heavily on your niche, your country's traffic, and how well your content ranks. But for a technical blog with decent search traffic, it's one of the easiest ways to start earning something while you build toward bigger opportunities like sponsorships or your own product.

This guide walks through the whole process — from setting up your account to getting approved to actually placing ads that don't ruin your site.

## Before you even sign up

Google reviews every site manually before turning ads on. Skipping this prep step is the number one reason developers get rejected on their first try.

Here's what your blog needs before you apply:

**A domain you own.** Free subdomains like `yourname.blogspot.com` or `.wordpress.com` (the free tier) can work in some cases, but a custom domain like `yourblog.com` looks more professional and gives you full control. If you're serious about this, buy a domain — it costs less than a coffee subscription per month.

**Real, original content.** You need enough posts that a reviewer can tell your site isn't empty. There's no official minimum, but somewhere between 15 and 30 solid articles is a safe range. Thin content, copied tutorials, or AI-generated filler with no personal insight will get flagged.

**A clean, working site.** No broken links, no "under construction" pages, no missing images. If your blog looks unfinished, Google assumes it isn't ready for ads either.

**Required pages.** You need at minimum:
- An About page explaining who you are and what the blog covers
- A Contact page or contact method
- A Privacy Policy page (this is not optional — AdSense requires it)

**Consistent traffic.** You don't need viral numbers, but a site with zero visitors for months looks abandoned. Some organic search traffic, even a trickle, helps your case.

**Age of the domain.** A brand-new domain isn't disqualifying, but older domains with a track record tend to get approved faster.

## Step 1: Create a Google Account

If you already use Gmail, Google Docs, or Google Drive, you have this covered. If not:

1. Go to `accounts.google.com`
2. Click **Create account**
3. Choose "For myself" or "To manage my business," depending on how you're running the blog
4. Fill in your name, choose a username, and set a password
5. Verify with your phone number
6. Finish the setup

It's worth using an account tied to your blog's identity rather than a personal throwaway account, especially if you plan to grow this into a real project.

## Step 2: Set up your AdSense account

1. Go to `adsense.google.com`
2. Click **Get started**
3. Sign in with the Google account from Step 1
4. Enter your website URL exactly as it appears in the browser (including `https://`)
5. Choose your country or territory — this matters for tax and payment purposes, and it usually can't be changed later, so double-check it
6. Review and accept the AdSense Terms & Conditions
7. Click **Create account**

At this point, Google creates your AdSense account but it isn't active for ads yet. You're now in the review queue.

## Step 3: Connect your site

Google needs proof that you actually own the site you submitted. You'll get a snippet of code that looks something like this:

```html
<script async src="https://pagead2.googlesyndication.com/pagead/js/adsbygoogle.js?client=ca-pub-XXXXXXXXXXXXXXXX"
     crossorigin="anonymous"></script>
```

Where to paste it depends on your platform:

**Self-hosted (WordPress, custom HTML, static site generators):** Paste this inside the `<head>` tag of every page. Most people do this through their theme's header file or a plugin like "Insert Headers and Footers" for WordPress.

**WordPress.com:** You'll need at least the Premium plan to add custom code, or you can use their built-in ad program instead.

**Static sites (Jekyll, Hugo, Next.js, etc.):** Drop the snippet into your base layout template so it loads on every page, not just the homepage.

Once it's added, go back to your AdSense dashboard and click **Verify**. If Google can detect the script, your site status will move from "not connected" to "getting ready."

## Step 4: Wait for the review

This is the part nobody enjoys. Review times vary — sometimes a few days, sometimes several weeks. Google is checking:

- That your content follows their policies (no adult content, no plagiarism, no content promoting illegal activity)
- That the site has real, navigable content
- That there's no fake traffic, click manipulation, or spammy behavior
- That your privacy policy and site structure are in order

During this period, don't panic-refresh your dashboard every hour. Instead, keep writing good content. A site that's actively growing during review looks better than one that went silent right after applying.

You'll get an email once a decision is made. It'll either say you're approved, or it'll list reasons for rejection.

## Common rejection reasons (and how to fix them)

**"Low value content"** — This usually means your posts are too short, too generic, or too similar to content already available elsewhere. Add depth: real code examples, screenshots, your own testing results, or opinions based on experience.

**"Site under construction"** — Even one placeholder page can trigger this. Audit every page on your site before reapplying.

**"Unable to review site"** — Sometimes this just means the crawler couldn't access your pages properly. Check your `robots.txt` file isn't blocking Google, and make sure your site loads fast and doesn't require login to view content.

**"Insufficient traffic"** — There's no hard number, but if your analytics show almost no visitors, focus on SEO and promotion first, then reapply.

**"Policy violations"** — Check for anything that could look like copied content, even accidentally. Tools like Copyscape can help you verify originality.

You can reapply once you've fixed the issues — there's no permanent penalty for a rejection, just a waiting period, usually around two weeks before resubmitting.

## Step 5: You're approved — now what?

Once approved, your account becomes active and you'll get access to the full AdSense dashboard. Now you decide how ads actually show up on your site.

### Auto ads vs manual ad units

**Auto ads** let Google's algorithm decide where and how many ads to place on each page. It's the fastest way to get started — just enable it and Google handles placement, format, and density automatically.

**Manual ad units** give you full control. You choose exactly where each ad block goes in your layout. This takes more setup work but produces a much better reading experience, especially for a technical blog where code blocks, diagrams, and long paragraphs need breathing room.

Most experienced bloggers start with Auto ads to see what Google suggests, then switch to manual placements once they understand their traffic and layout better.

### Creating a manual ad unit

1. In your AdSense dashboard, go to **Ads** → **By ad unit**
2. Click **Display ads** (this is the most common and flexible type)
3. Name the unit something you'll recognize later, like "In-article-top" or "Sidebar-main"
4. Choose the size: **Responsive** is usually the safest choice since it adapts to different screen widths automatically
5. Click **Create**
6. Copy the generated code snippet
7. Paste it into the exact spot on your page where you want the ad to appear

### Where to actually place ads on a tech blog

This is where a lot of developers get it wrong. Cramming ads everywhere kills the reading experience and can even violate policy. A few placements that tend to work well without being intrusive:

- **After the introduction**, before the main content starts
- **Mid-article**, ideally after a natural breakpoint like the end of a section, never inside a code block
- **In the sidebar**, if your layout has one, since it doesn't interrupt reading flow
- **At the end of the article**, where readers who finished the post are done reading anyway

Avoid placing ads:
- Right next to buttons or links that could cause accidental clicks
- Inside or immediately touching code snippets
- Stacked back-to-back with little content between them
- Above the fold in a way that pushes your actual content down

## Step 6: Set up payments

You won't get paid until your earnings cross a threshold (commonly around $10 just to verify your address, and around $100 to actually receive a payment — these thresholds vary slightly by country).

1. Go to **Payments** in your AdSense dashboard
2. Add your payment information — bank details or another supported payout method depending on your country
3. Verify your address — Google mails a PIN code to your registered address once you cross the initial earnings threshold; you'll need to enter this PIN to unlock payments
4. Fill in tax information, which is required even if you don't owe anything, since Google needs this for compliance

Payments are typically issued monthly, once you clear the payout threshold, with a delay of a few weeks after the earning month closes.

## Step 7: Keep an eye on performance

Your AdSense dashboard shows metrics like:

- **Page RPM** — how much you earn per 1,000 page views
- **CTR (click-through rate)** — the percentage of viewers who click an ad
- **Impressions** — how many times ads were shown

For developer-focused content, RPM tends to be decent because tech and software advertisers often pay well for that audience. Don't obsess over daily numbers — focus on writing content that ranks and brings in organic search traffic, since traffic volume is what moves the needle far more than tweaking ad placement.

## A few things that trip people up later

**Never click your own ads.** Even "testing" by clicking is against policy and can get your account banned permanently, with no way to appeal.

**Don't ask others to click your ads.** This includes friends, family, or asking readers directly. It's treated the same as fraud.

**Watch your page speed.** Too many ad scripts can slow your site down, which hurts both your search ranking and your ad revenue, since slower pages usually mean fewer impressions.

**Mobile matters.** A large share of blog traffic is mobile. Make sure your ad units look fine on small screens and don't push your content off-screen.

**Review your policy center regularly.** AdSense has a "Policy center" tab that flags issues before they become serious. Checking it occasionally can save you from an unexpected account suspension.

## Final thoughts

Getting approved for AdSense isn't about tricking an algorithm — it's about having a real site with real content that Google feels comfortable putting ads on. If you focus on writing genuinely useful technical content first, the approval process tends to go a lot smoother, and the ads you add afterward will feel like a natural part of the site rather than something bolted on.

Once you're up and running, treat AdSense as one small piece of a bigger monetization strategy. Combine it with things like affiliate links for developer tools, your own digital products, or sponsorships as your traffic grows. Ads alone rarely carry a blog — but they're a solid, low-effort starting point while you build the rest.
